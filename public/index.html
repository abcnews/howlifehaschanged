How life has changed for people your age

Catherine Hanrahan and Joshua Byrd Tue 11 Dec 2018, 3:50 PM AEDT

What's different about your life, compared to someone who was your age in the 1980s?

Big hair, fluoro colours, Rubik's cubes and Walkmans â€” the '80s feel like another country. And in some ways, they are.

How we are educated, work, marry, have families, spend money and look after our health have fundamentally changed since then.

Take a look at how some of those changes have affected people at different stages of their lives.

Children

Babies have a much higher chance of surviving their first years of life now than in the 80s and that's partly due to big drops in conditions like sudden infant death syndrome (SIDS).

The rate of babies dying in their first year has fallen 69 per cent

The SIDS death rate is 95 per cent lower

Kids in the 80s were more likely to be looked after in the home and 21st century parents spend more money on childcare now.

More kids under 12 attend childcare

Weekly spending on childcare is six times higher

Australia was still becoming accustomed to working mothers in 1981. "Children grow fat while mothers work," one headline screamed.

Both parents are now working in the majority of families

The problem of obesity in kids has only gotten worse, not better, since then.

One in four children are now overweight or obese

Teenagers

Teenagers in the 80s weren't keen on staying at school and reported being 'fed up' with it and wanting their own money.

Nearly all teenagers now stay at school to year 12

Teenagers who have full-time jobs now earn more

Australia had a 'youth job crisis' in 1981, according to the government. But it's actually harder to find full-time jobs now if you're a teenager. Unemployment in this group peaked at 34 per cent in 1992.

There are now also more teens and twenty-somethings who want more hours of work, meaning they are 'under-employed'.

Among teens who want to work full-time, more of them can't find a job now

Three times more young people are under-employed

More worrying for 80s teens was the 'horrific' road toll in their age group, with drink-driving blamed.

The road accident death rate for young Australians is five times lower

People in their 20s

'20: too young to get married?' the Sydney Morning Herald asked in 1981. It wasn't for Lady Diana Spencer, who at 19, married Prince Charles that year.

But these days, it's definitely too young for most Australians to get married.

Only one in four 25 to 29-year-olds are married now

The median marriage age has gone up eight years

Later marrying age, and the cost of housing, means more twenty-somethings still live at home now.

One in three 18 to 34-year-olds live with their parents now

The inflation rate was six times higher in 1981 and was blamed for students being 'caught in a poverty slide', and that was without escalating 21st century rents to deal with.

Weekly rent has more than doubled since 1981

Fortnightly student living-away-from-home allowance is 16 per cent higher now

In the same time period, the dole has only gone up slightly.

Singles unemployment benefit is only 20 per cent higher

People in their 30s

Couples living in de facto relationships were not granted the same financial and property rights as married couples until 1985.

The way 30-somethings deal with their relationships has been transformed since then.

De facto relationships are much more common

As women have become more educated, they are also having children later.

A university education is much more common

Women are having their first baby about four years later

Australians in their thirties find it hard to get into the property market â€” the median house price has leapt since 1981.

And affordability â€” mortgage payments as a percentage of household income â€” peaked at 38 per cent in 1989.

The median house price is more than twice as high

[21-medianhouseprice.png]

Mortgage repayments as a per cent of income are also higher

Thirty-somethings are far less likely to own their home now, but it's not because they are spending all their money on recreation.

Less than half own their home now

Proportion of weekly spending on recreation has gone down

People in their 40s

More 40-something women have university educations now and not surprisingly, large families are becoming much more unusual.

Having a university education is six times more common

The proportion of women with four or more children has plummeted

In 1981, newspapers were warning women about 'when that extra pay upsets the marriage'. These days, marriage doesn't mean staying out of the workforce.

Lots of married women didn't work in 1981

'Life is tougher for the average family', said the headlines in 1981.

Since then, household incomes and spending have both climbed.

Households spend $464 more every week

Average gross household income per week is $740 more

Eighteen per cent of household spending now goes to housing, up from 9 per cent in 1984.

Housing now makes up a fifth of weekly household spending

People in their 50s

In 1981, the manufacturing industry was the biggest employer of men in their 50s, though it had already been declining for a decade. That trend hasn't stopped.

Nor has the decline in trade union membership.

Only one in ten 55 to 59-year-old men now work in manufacturing

At least half of all employees used to be in a trade union

Australians in their 50s have cut back on at least some unhealthy habits since the 1980s.

The proportion of 45 to 54-year-old men who smoke has halved

Pure alcohol consumption has fallen by a third

Australians were being warned in 1985 that anger caused heart attacks. But it's actually been healthier living that has contributed to a big reduction in heart disease and strokes around the age of 50.

The heart disease death rate has fallen 80 per cent

The stroke death rate has fallen 70 per cent

But being overweight is now the norm.

More than three quarters are now overweight or obese

In 1984, the government launched an advertising campaign to 'mend marriages'. Divorce rates are higher now for over 40s, but lower for younger people.

Divorces are more common place

People in their 60s

The good news for today's 60-somethings is they can expect to live longer than people who were the same age in the 1980s, partly due to a massive reduction in heart attacks.

People aged 65 can expect to live an extra five years

The heart attack death rate has fallen 90 per cent

That might be partly related to a big drop in smoking rates.

The proportion of people who smoke is down

The lung disease death rate has fallen even faster

It might be just as well they're living longer, because people in their 60s are less likely to be retired now.

Fewer 65 to 69-year-olds are retired now

The average retirement age has gone up by about three years

At the start of the 1980s, very few people in their 60s had a university education. But more people were earning big money without one.

Far more 60-64-year-olds have at least a Bachelor's degree now

In 1981, more than half those earning more than $100,000 a year had only a high school education

People aged 70 and older

Retirees have more income these days than in the 1980s, when only half of all employees had superannuation coverage.

Most people 65 and over lived on the equivalent of less than $15,000 per year in 1981

Three times more of those aged 65 and over now have an income above $65,000 per year

Household spending on heating and electricity has gone up a bit since the 80s, but spending on health has doubled.

Those 65 and over spend $10 a week more on fuel and power now

The same age group spends $50 a week more on health care now

Pensioners have more money in real terms compared to the 80s.

The annual singles pension payment is 60 per cent higher now

Average weekly household income for those aged 65 and over is up 65 per cent

Slightly fewer of those aged 65 and over own their own home outright now

Life expectancy has also climbed significantly.

The proportion of people aged 85 and over has more than doubled

The cancer death rate has fallen

A lower proportion of people aged 75 and over are widowed

About the data

  • Infant mortality data came from the OECD
  • SIDS death rates were supplied by Red Nose
  • Childcare attendance represents children under 12 in formal childcare; data came from the ABS Child Care Arrangements, Australia, 1984 and Childhood Education and Care, Australia, June 2017 studies
  • Total weekly household spending, gross household income, average weekly household income and spending on childcare, rent, housing, fuel and power, healthcare and home ownership rates came from the ABS Household Expenditure Surveys from 1984 and 2015-16
  • Data on children with parents working came from the ABS Child Care Arrangements, Australia, 1984 and Labour Force, Australia: Labour Force Status and Other Characteristics of Families, June 2016 studies; 1981 data included families with children under 12
  • Children's obesity data for 1985 came from an Australian study (7-15-year-olds) and for 2016 from the AIHW's "A picture of overweight and obesity in Australia 2017" report (5-17-year-olds)
  • School retention rates came from the ABS's Social Indicators Australia 1984 and Schools, Australia, 2017 reports
  • Teenagers' (15-19-yr-olds) median annual income and trade union membership rates came from the ABS's Social Indicators Australia 1984 and Characteristics of Employment, Australia, August 2016 reports
  • Unemployed, underemployed and married people not in workforce rates by age group came from the ABS's Labour Force, Australia, October 2018 report for either June and May
  • Fatal road accident rates were supplied by the Department of Infrastructure, Regional Development and Cities
  • Marriage rates, Bachelor's degree rates, women with four or more children, people working in manufacturing, yearly incomes and age proportions came from ABS census data from 1981 and 2016
  • Earnings over $100,000 by education level is based on those earning more than $98,277 (nominally $26,001) in 1981 and $104,000 in 2016
  • Median marriage age came from the ABS's Social Indicators Australia 1984 and Marriages and Divorces, Australia, 2016 reports
  • Proportions of 18-34-year-olds living at home was calculated from census data by the ABS
  • Student living-away-from-home allowance came from the Sydney Morning Herald, September 14, 1981 and the Department of Human Services
  • Unemployment benefit and pension payments came from the Australian Government Social Security Guide
  • De facto relationship and widowed rates came from the ABS's Social Indicators Australia 1984 and the 2016 census
  • Median age of first time mothers came from the ABS's Social Indicators Australia 1984 and the AIHW's Australia's mothers and babies 2016 reports
  • National median house price is the weighted average of six capitals, and mortgage affordability is mortgage repayments at the standard variable rate on 75 per cent of the median house price on a 25 year loan as a percentage of average household disposable income, and was supplied by Bis Oxford Economics
  • Home ownership rates for 25-34-year-olds represent households where the home is owned and the reference person, who is typically, but not always, the owner, is aged 25-34. The data was supplied by the ABS
  • Alcohol consumption rates came from the ABS's Apparent Consumption of Alcohol, Australia, 2016-17 report
  • Smoking rates, death rates from heart disease (ischemic heart disease), strokes (cerebrovascular disease), cancer and lung disease came from the ABS's Social Indicators Australia 1984 and Causes of Death, Australia, 2016 reports
  • Adult obesity data came from the ABS's National Health Surveys for 1989-90 and 2014-15
  • Divorce rates came from the ABS's Marriages and Divorces, Australia, 2016 report
  • Retirement rates represent people not in the workforce and came from the ABS's Labour Force Australia January 1981 and Retirement and Retirement Intentions, Australia, July 2016 to June 2017
  • Average retirement age and life expectancy at 65 came from the OECD
  • Incomes of people aged 65 and over was based on earning more than $68,042 ($18,001 nominally) in 1981 and $65,000 in 2016, or less than $15,123 ($4,000 nominally) in 1981 and $15,599 in 2016
