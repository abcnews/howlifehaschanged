How life has changed

Wed 14 Nov 2018, 6:14 PM AEDT

This is some header text for a test.

Big hair, fluro colours, Rubik's cubes and Walkmans - the 80s feels like another country.

How we are educated, work, marry, have children, spend money and look after our health have fundamentally changed since then.

We've crunched the numbers on the social and economic demographics for each age group. Click on the buttons below to see 'How life has changed' since the 1980s.

Children

Babies have a much higher chance of surviving their first years of life now than in the 80s and that's partly due to big drops in conditions like Sudden Infant Death Syndrome.

The proportion of babies dying in their first year is 70 per cent lower

The SIDS death rate is 90 per cent lower than in the 80s

Kids in the 80s were more likely to be looked after in the home and twenty-first century parents spend more money on childcare now.

More kids under 12 are in out-of-home childcare

Weekly spending on childcare is six times higher (in 2016 dollars)

Australia was still becoming accustomed to working mothers in 1981. 'Children grow fat while mothers work', the headlines screamed.

Both parents are now working in the majority of families with kids under 15

But the problem of obesity in kids has got worse, not better, since then.

One in four 7-15-year-olds are now overweight or obese

Teenagers

Teenagers in the 80s weren't keen on staying at school and reported being 'fed up' with it and wanting their own money.

Nearly all teenagers now finish Year 12

The teenagers who have full time jobs now earn more (in 2016 dollars)

Australia had a 'youths job crisis' in 1981 according to the government, though it's actually harder to find a full-time job now if you are a teenager.

Unemployment in this group peaked at 34 per cent in 1992.

There are now also more teens and twenty-somethings 'under-employed', who want more hours work.

The proportion of unemployed 15-19 year-olds looking for a full time job is higher

Three times more 15-24-year-olds are under-employed

More worrying for 80s teens was the 'horrific' road accident death rate in their age group, with drink-driving blamed.

The road accident death rate in 15-25-year-olds is five times lower now

Twenties

'20: too young to get married?' the Sydney Morning Herald asked in 1981.

Not for Lady Diana Spencer, who at 19, married Prince Charles that year.

Only one in four 25-29-year-olds are married now

The median marriage age has gone up eight years

Later marrying age, and the cost of housing, means more twenty-somethings live at home now.

One in three 18-34-year-olds live with their parents now

The inflation rate was six times higher in 1981 and was blamed for students being 'caught in a poverty slide', and that was without twenty-first century rents to deal with.

Weekly rent has more than doubled since 1981

Fortnightly student living-away-from-home allowance is three times higher now (in 2016 dollars)

Since then, the dole has only gone up slightly.

Singles unemployment benefit is only 20 per cent higher

Thirties

Women in de facto relationships should be called mistresses or girlfriends, not wives, a Hobart magistrate declared in 1984.

Couples 'living in sin' were not granted the same financial and property rights as married couples until 1985.

Very few 30-34-year-olds lived in de facto relationships in 1981

As women have become more educated, they are having children later.

A quarter of 30-34-year-olds now have at least a Bachelor's degree

The median age of first-time mothers has gone up four years

Australians in their thirties find it hard to get on the property ladder - the median house price has leapt since 1981.

But affordability - mortgage payments as a percentage of household income, peaked in 1989.

The median house price is more than twice as high (in 2016 dollars)

Mortgage repayments as a percent of income are also higher

Forties

More forty-something women have university educations now and not surprisingly, large families are unusual.

Six times more 40-44-year-olds have at least a Bachelor's degree

Few 45-49-year-old women now have four or more children

Marriage doesn't mean staying out of the workforce in the twenty-first century, though in 1981 women were warned about 'when that extra pay upsets the marriage'.

Lots of 45-49-year-old married women didn't work in 1981

'Life is tougher for the average family', said the headlines in 1981.

Wages were up 75 per cent in six years but rampant inflation meant basic living costs had risen by 100 per cent.

Households spend $464 more every week (in 2016 dollars)

Average gross household income per week is $740 more (in 2016 dollars)

Twenty per cent of household spending now goes to housing, up from 13 per cent in 1984.

Housing now makes up a third of weekly household spending

Fifties

In 1981, the manufacturing industry was the biggest employer of men in their fifties, though it had been declining for a decade.

Only one in ten 55-59-year-old men now work in manufacturing

At least half of employees used to be in a trade union

Australians have cut back on at least some unhealthy habits since the 1980s.

The proportion of 45-54-year-olds who smoke has more than halved

Pure alcohol consumption per year has fallen by 30 per cent (litres)

And that has contributed to a big reduction in heart attacks and strokes around the age of fifty, although Australians were warned in 1985 that anger caused heart attacks.

The heart attack death rate has fallen 80 per cent in per 45-54-year-olds

The stroke death rate has fallen 70 per cent in per 45-54-year-olds

But being overweight is now the norm.

More than three quarters of 45-64-year-olds are now overweight or obese

In 1984, the government launched an advertising campaign to 'mend marriages'.

Divorce rates are higher now for over 40s, but lower for younger people.

Divorce rates are 80 per cent higher for 55-59-year-olds

Sixties

It was far easier to earn big money in the 80s armed only with a high school education.

In 1981, more than half those earning more than $100,000 a year had only a high school education

Far more 60-64-year-olds have at least a Bachelor's degree now

In 1985, a NSW court awarded a female sales assistant damages when her employer forced her to retire at 60.

The pension eligibility age for women began to increase up to 65 from 1995.

Less 65-69-year-olds are retired now

The average retirement age has gone up by about three years

Sixty-five year old's can now expect to live five years longer than retirees in the 80s.

The massive reduction in heart attacks has contributed to that.

Now 65-year-olds can expect to live another 20 years

The heart attack death rate has fallen 90 per cent in 65-74-year-olds

A Sydney restaurant introduced 'non-smoking Tuesdays' in 1981 and a survey found 80 per cent wanted smoking banned in doctor's waiting rooms.

The proportion of 55-64-year-olds who smoke has dropped by 40 per cent

The lung disease death rate has fallen 80 per cent in 65-74-year-olds

Seventies and over

Retirees in 2016 have more income than in the 80s, when only half of all employees had superannuation coverage.

Most people 65 and over lived on less than $15,000 per year in 1981 (in 2016 dollars)

Three times more of those aged 65 and over now have an income over $65,000 per year (in 2016 dollars)

Household spending on power has gone up a bit since the 80s, but spending on health has doubled.

Those 65 and over spend $10 a week more on fuel and power now (in 2016 dollars)

Those 65 and over spend $50 a week more on health care now (in 2016 dollars)

Pensioners have more money in real terms compared to the 80s.

The annual singles pension payment is 60 per cent higher now (in 2016 dollars)

Average weekly household income for those aged 65 and over is up 65 per cent (in 2016 dollars)

Life expectancy has gone up and the proportion of the population aged over 85 has more than doubled since the 80s.

The proportion of those aged 85 and over has more than doubled

Less people are dying from cancer now

Older people would need to rely more on government and less on family for aged care, a news story noted in 1982.

That's because there were less children to care for their parents and the pool of unmarried middle-aged women was smaller, it said.

Less people aged 75 and over are widowed

Slightly less of those aged 65 and over own their own home outright now
